### The "Pass Culture" Phenomenon (2021)

France launched the "Pass Culture" (Culture Pass) — a government app giving every 18-year-old 300 euros (~$350) to spend on cultural activities. The result shocked the establishment:

- **75% of all purchases** through the app were books
- **~Two-thirds of book purchases were manga**
- French media renamed it the **"Manga Pass"**
- Top titles purchased: Jujutsu Kaisen, Attack on Titan, One Piece, Demon Slayer
- Young people would leave bookshops with "10, 20, or even 50 manga under their arm"
- The program was one of President Macron's campaign promises for cultural engagement
- Bookstore owners were delighted — the program drove teens into independent shops rather than Amazon

### Club Dorothee — How It Shaped a Generation

**Overview:**
- French TV program on TF1, aired from **September 2, 1987 to August 31, 1997**
- Produced by AB Productions
- Hosted by Dorothee, Jacky, Ariane Carletti, Francois Corbier, and Patrick Simpson-Jones
- Featured over **127 anime series** during its run

**Cultural Impact:**
- Before Club Dorothee, **Japanese animation was practically invisible in France**
- AB Productions made massive purchases of anime at negligible prices — content that nobody else wanted to touch
- For an entire generation, the gateway to Japanese culture **was not the Internet but TF1**
- The show revolutionized French television by popularizing Japanese anime with young audiences
- Anime broadcast by Club Dorothee conveyed values of willpower, friendship, and self-sacrifice that resonated with French youth
- France is now the **second largest consumer of anime in the world after Japan** — a direct legacy of Club Dorothee

**Key Anime Broadcast:** The show introduced French children to Dragon Ball, Saint Seiya (Les Chevaliers du Zodiaque), Captain Tsubasa (Olive et Tom), Fist of the North Star, and many more.

### French Manga Artists / Creators Influenced by Japanese Style

**The "Manfra" Movement (French-made manga):**

1. **Tony Valente — "Radiant"**
   - French comic series in manga style, published by Ankama Editions starting July 2013
   - 19 volumes released as of research date
   - **First manfra to be published in Japan** (2015), with help from Japanese manga artists Yusuke Murata and Hiro Mashima
   - Received a Japanese anime adaptation by Lerche studio (broadcast on NHK)
   - Valente works as a one-man team on story, art, and colors

2. **Reno Lemaire — "Dreamland"**
   - One of the first successful French manga series, launched in 2006
   - Also published in Japan — a major achievement for a non-Japanese creator
   - The series has run for about 15+ years

3. **Frederic Boilet — "La Nouvelle Manga" movement**
   - French manga artist who debuted in 1983
   - Blends European bande dessinee with Japanese manga styles
   - Seminal work: "Yukiko's Spinach" (2001)
   - Coined/promoted the concept of "nouvelle manga" — a cross-cultural movement between French BD and Japanese manga

4. **Bastien Vives**
   - French artist influenced by Japanese manga, works on martial arts comics in manga style
   - Uses a production system particular to the Japanese manga world (~20 pages per week)
   - References Bakuman as creative inspiration

**The broader movement:** "La nouvelle manga" sees French and Japanese creators collaborate to merge the best of bande dessinee and manga, creating "manfra" — French-made manga-esque comics.

## TOPIC 2: LINE IN THAILAND — WHY DEEP LOCALIZATION?

### Why LINE Succeeded in Thailand While WhatsApp Dominates Elsewhere

**1. First-Mover Advantage in the Right Moment:**
- LINE launched in Thailand in **2012**, gaining an early foothold
- LINE originated as a **disaster response app** during the 2011 Tohoku earthquake/tsunami in Japan
- Designed to use internet-based connections rather than damaged telecom infrastructure
- Within 18 months of creation, hit **100 million downloads** globally
- WhatsApp never prioritized Thailand for early marketing or partnerships

**2. Cultural Alignment:**
- LINE allows communication **without revealing personal phone numbers** (via QR code or username) — aligning with Thai/Japanese cultural preference for privacy
- Thai culture is **high-context** — non-verbal cues matter as much as literal meaning
- LINE's sticker system resonated deeply with Thai expressive communication style
- WhatsApp's text-centric approach was too utilitarian for Thai communicators

**3. Super-App Strategy vs. Single-Purpose Messaging:**
- WhatsApp focused purely on messaging
- LINE built a **comprehensive digital ecosystem** in Thailand:
  - **LINE TV** — became #1 TV re-run platform in Thailand
  - **LINE TODAY** — became #1 news portal with 32 million MAUs
  - **LINE MAN** — on-demand delivery (food, packages, 24-hour service via motorcycle fleet)
  - **LINE Pay** (formerly Rabbit LINE Pay) — mobile payments
  - **LINE SHOPPING** — e-commerce
  - **LINE BK** — digital banking
  - **LINE Official Account** — business communication
  - **LINE STICKERS** — creative expression
  - **LINE MELODY** — ringtones
  - **LINE OPENCHAT** — community forums
  - **LINE VOOM** — social content feed
  - **LINE WEBTOON** — digital comics
  - **LINE GAME** — gaming platform
  - **LINE HEALTH** (new 2025) — health services
  - **LINE GIFT** (new 2025) — gifting platform
  - **LINE PREMIUM** (new 2025) — subscription tier

**4. Why WhatsApp Didn't Do the Same:**
- WhatsApp's global strategy was one-size-fits-all minimalism
- WhatsApp never invested in local marketing or partnerships in Thailand
- WhatsApp's first-mover advantage was "whittled away by Asian chat apps' more comprehensive offerings"
- Meta/Facebook's ownership meant WhatsApp had different strategic priorities (global scale over local depth)

## TOPIC 3: POKEMON — SEPARATE JAPAN VS US DEVELOPMENT NARRATIVES

### Pokemon in Japan: Timeline from Game Boy to National Phenomenon

**1996: The Birth**
- **February 27, 1996:** Pokemon Red and Green released on Game Boy in Japan
- Created by Satoshi Tajiri and developed by Game Freak
- Nintendo had **no high expectations**; media largely ignored the games
- **April 1996:** CoroCoro Comic magazine announced the "Legendary Pokemon Offer" featuring mysterious Pokemon #151 (Mew)
  - 78,000 fans sent in applications; only **20 were selected** to receive Mew
  - This created massive word-of-mouth buzz
- **By September 1996:** Sales surpassed **1 million units**
- **October 20, 1996:** First Pokemon Trading Card Game set released (102 cards)
- **1996 total:** Red, Green, and Blue sold **1.04 million units combined**

**1997: Anime Launch & National Phenomenon**
- **April 1, 1997:** Pokemon anime series premiered on TV Tokyo
- By December 1997, **55% of kindergarten, elementary, and middle school-aged children** in Tokyo's Kawasaki district were watching Pokemon weekly
- In Toyohashi city, viewership reached **70% of elementary school students**
- The anime held the **highest ratings for its time slot**, watched by ~4.6 million households
- **1997 game sales:** 3.65 million additional units
- **December 16, 1997:** The "Porygon Incident" — Episode 38 ("Electric Soldier Porygon") caused photosensitive epileptic seizures
  - Over **685 people hospitalized**, mostly children
  - Anime went on **four-month hiatus**
  - Paradoxically, the incident increased global awareness of Pokemon

**1998: Peak in Japan**
- Anime returned April 16, 1998
- **By 1998:** 10 million units sold in Japan
- Pokemon Red, Green, and Blue ultimately sold **10.23 million copies** in Japan — the country's best-selling video game until November 2022
- **By March 1999:** Generating revenues of nearly **¥400 billion ($3.28 billion) annually**

### Pokemon in the USA: How It Was Brought to America

**Localization Strategy:**
- **4Kids Entertainment** licensed anime, TV, home video, and merchandise rights for Pokemon outside Asia from **1998 to 2005**
- 4Kids sub-licensed the animated series to partners in Canada, Latin America, and Europe
- 4Kids also served as licensing agent for merchandise on behalf of Nintendo of America
- Games were localized by Nintendo Treehouse (in-house localization team)

**US Launch Timeline:**
- **September 8, 1998:** Localized anime premiered in first-run syndication — **20 days before** game release
  - This was strategic: build awareness through free TV, then sell the games
- **September 28, 1998:** Pokemon Red and Blue released in North America
  - Became **fastest-selling Game Boy game** — 200,000 copies in first two weeks
  - **4 million units** sold by end of 1998
- **December 1998:** Pokemon anime became the **highest-rated syndicated children's show** during weekdays
- **February 13, 1999:** Pokemon launched on **Kids' WB** national TV block
  - First episode became **most-watched premiere in Kids' WB history**
  - By November, Nielsen reported "half the boys (ages 6-11) watching TV at 10am are seeing Pokemon"
  - Hit a **3.9 rating** in first week, reaching 3.1 million viewers

**Pokemania Peak (November 1999):**
- November 1999 estimated as **peak of Pokemania** in the US
- **Pokemon: The First Movie** released November 12, 1999
  - Grossed **$172 million worldwide** at box office
  - Sold **10 million home video units** in US (4.2 million VHS = $58.8 million)
- Burger King promotion — **one of the largest** in fast-food history
- Nintendo saw **250% increase in profits** in 1999 vs. prior year
- Pokemon franchise accounted for **over 30% of Nintendo's revenue** in 1999
- 4Kids, initially a "little-known firm," **expanded 30x in revenues**
  - Named **fastest-growing company in America** by Fortune magazine (September 4, 2000 issue)

**Decline:**
- By summer 2001, Pokemon shelf space was "a fraction of what it enjoyed in fall 1999"
- By end of 2001, Pokemania was fading globally
- But franchise continued as a steady, profitable business rather than a fad

### Pokemon Global Spread — Region by Region

**Europe:**
- **October 5, 1999:** Pokemon Red and Blue released in Europe (one year after US)
- UK was the first major European market — games and anime both arrived in 1999
- Anime aired on **CITV block** (within SMTV Live programme) in the UK
- **Pokemon: The First Movie** premiered in European countries **February 2000**
- France, Germany, Spain, Italy all received localized versions
- Europe was the second wave after the US had proven the concept

**Latin America:**
- **October 1998:** Red and Blue released in English first
- **April 26, 1999:** Anime premiered in Chilean channel Chillevision (*Pokemon, Yo te elijo!*)
- **June 14, 1999:** Anime premiered on Mexican channel Canal 5
- **September 6, 1999:** Pan-regional cable debut on **Cartoon Network Latinoamerica**, reaching all of Latin America and the Caribbean
- Became one of the most popular shows on Cartoon Network Latin America

**Southeast Asia:**
- Anime broadcast followed an "East Asian pipeline" — from Japanese to Korean, Greater China, Thai, and occasionally Indonesian and Vietnamese dubs
- **2006:** Pokemon Trading Figure Game launched in Southeast Asia (by Kaiyodo)
- **2006:** Pokemon Korea, Inc. established; later offices opened in **Singapore** for Southeast Asian oversight
- Pokemon had organic fan communities in Southeast Asia before official distribution

**China (Complicated History):**
- China enacted a **video game console ban in 2000** (fears about negative effects on youth development)
- Pokemon was **never officially sold in China** during this period
- Despite the ban, China had a **significant fanbase** through overseas purchases, counterfeit versions, and smuggling
- **2015:** Console ban lifted
- **2019:** Nintendo partnered with **Tencent** to bring the Switch to China
- Tencent's **TiMi Studios** developed **Pokemon Unite** (MOBA game)
- **2021:** New Pokemon Snap became the **first Pokemon game officially released in China**
- **Pokemon GO banned in China since 2017** — government claims "threat to information security"
- **December 2022:** Pokemon Unite approved by National Press and Publication Administration

## KEY ANALYTICAL OBSERVATIONS

### Cross-Case Patterns

1. **Club Dorothee (France) / Pokemania (USA):** Both show how a dedicated children's TV programming window can create generational cultural imprinting. Club Dorothee ran 1987-1997; the Pokemon anime launched in US 1998. Both created lifelong consumers.

2. **LINE Thailand / Pokemon in China:** Both show how platform/franchise success depends heavily on regulatory and cultural environment. LINE succeeded by deeply adapting; Pokemon struggled in China due to the console ban and regulatory barriers.

3. **The "Super-App" Factor:** LINE Thailand's success mirrors how Pokemon became a "super-franchise" — both succeeded by being ecosystems rather than single products. LINE is not just messaging; Pokemon is not just a game.

4. **France's Pass Culture = Natural Experiment:** The French government's $350 cultural credit program proved that manga preference among youth was organic and deep, not manufactured. When given free choice, 75% bought manga.

5. **Revenue Concentration:** In all three cases, merchandise/licensed goods dominate revenue:
   - Pokemon: ~$100B of $150B total (67%) from merchandise
   - LINE Thailand: Stickers (cultural merchandise) are a key differentiator
   - France Manga: Physical volumes drive the market (not digital)
